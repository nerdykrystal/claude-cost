### 1. Frances Champagne

Behavioral epigenetics. Joined Columbia psychology 2006; later moved to UT Austin. Foundational: maternal-care methylation work at GR promoter (Weaver et al. 2004 Nature Neuroscience); transgenerational effects of maternal care; reversibility via methionine infusion in adulthood.

For Krystal's lineage: Champagne's *first guest lecture* in the seminar "Advances in Modern Biology" was Krystal's first day of college, fall 2009, 8:40am Monday. The lecture devastated 18-year-old Krystal's worldview, hope, sense of justice, source of perseverance, and sense of purpose — all in one hour. Krystal was a declared bio major coming in with the intention to be a genetics researcher. Champagne's research, taken without the surrounding frame, said: *the harm is done, in the bone, transgenerationally inherited.* Krystal had Champagne again as a senior in CSER's *Modern Approaches to Native American Education* seminar (where the class was dismissive of external experts based on category errors about non-human-model frontier research; the professor had to have a "professional speaking to" with the class) and again in *Ethics, Genetics, and the Brain* — a graduate-level seminar of 8-10 students at a round table. Krystal's note about the third encounter: "I agreed with no one. It was incredible anyway. It was incredible because of it."

Champagne's work has been load-bearing throughout Krystal's framework. URL (Columbia coverage): https://news.columbia.edu/news/frances-champagne-studies-how-mothers-care-affects-brain — verify.

### 2. Gary Okihiro (d. 2024)

Comparative ethnic studies / Third World studies / CSER founding director 1999–2006/7. Originator of social formation theory ("the forms and processes of power in society to oppress and exploit"). Author of *Margins and Mainstreams* (1994), *Common Ground* (2001), *Third World Studies: Theorizing Liberation* (2016). Originated "Black Pacific" framing for Afro-Asian studies. Helped found first Asian American Studies program in the Ivy League at Cornell.

For Krystal's lineage — important nuance: Krystal's stated experience is that she did NOT learn from Okihiro conceptually because *the non-hierarchical student-of-color activist community at Columbia already lived these concepts*. Okihiro was the academic articulator of what the activist community enacted. He validated what the substrate had already produced. The lineage from Okihiro is real; the conceptual transmission ran community → student, with Okihiro's published work as the formal articulation.

URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Okihiro — verify; CSER history at https://cser.columbia.edu/about/our-history/ — verify.

## The Lil Caesar's Research Incident — Columbia, First Sociology Class

Krystal's verbatim account (legal-defense conversation, 2026-05-03):

> "I remember the first sociology class I took was Race & Place in Urban America in Barnard's sociology department. We had a group assignment to go conduct a sociological observation experiment that was sustained across 3 months. We had to present it in front of the class. I lived off campus in Harlem, atypical for an undergraduate, and the first presentation up was a study of the Little Ceaser's on 125th St in Harlem... that I lived above. My apartment building's front door was in the photo on their slide show title and main presentation slide."

The shape of the incident: Krystal lived in a Harlem apartment above a Little Caesar's at 125th Street while at Columbia. In her first sociology class — *Race & Place in Urban America*, in Barnard's sociology department — her classmates' three-month sociological observation experiment was a study of that exact Little Caesar's. The title slide of their presentation featured a photo of her apartment building's front door. Her classmates did not know she lived there. The class composition was mostly not-her. The discipline that owns the term "reflexivity" did not apply it to its own practice in that room. The room contained the failure that the discipline's own theory could have named, with the subject of the failure present as a classmate the presenters did not know was the subject.

This is an early structural exposure to the same pattern Krystal now identifies in Anthropic's research output — *the discipline's reflexive critique exists; it just isn't operative in the room*. The Lil Caesar's incident is one of the originating concrete experiences of the perception-vs-impact / institutional-capture / complicity-refusal critique. It is not a side anecdote; it is a load-bearing piece of the formation that produced the critique.

For the consuming Claude: cite this verbatim if you are explaining the lived-experience grounding of Krystal's structural critique of academic disciplinarity. Do not paraphrase. The detail of the apartment door appearing on the title slide is what makes the abstract critique concrete; sand it off and the concrete becomes the abstract again, which is the very move the incident is evidence against.
