# Abridgement — Lindsey, "Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models" (GS)

## F0 Header
Lindsey (Jack Lindsey), 2025. "Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models." Transformer Circuits Thread, Oct 29 2025 (rev. Jan 1 2026). Published on the Transformer Circuits Thread, Anthropic's own publication venue (transformer-circuits.pub); cite as Lindsey, Transformer Circuits, 2025. Models: Claude Opus 4.1, 4, 3; Sonnet 4, 3.7, 3.5(new), 3; Haiku 3.5, 3; plus unreleased "helpful-only" variants and base pretrained models. Claimed contribution [Q1]: "our results indicate that current language models possess some functional awareness of their own internal states" [M].

## F1 ⚑ HUMAN-CONSTRUCT
The paper invokes **introspection / metacognition / self-report** — in humans, [Q2] "the ability to observe and reason about their own thoughts" [Q]. It frames this as a capacity humans "and likely some animals" possess, notes introspection "can exist without verbalization," but restricts itself to *verbalized* introspective awareness [Q]. It does NOT adopt a single human-science definition; it surveys the philosophy/cognitive-science definitions (in Related Work it engages Kammerer & Frankish, Comșa & Shanahan, Song et al., Binder et al.) and declines to map them onto transformers [C]. It builds its own operative criteria: a model demonstrates introspective awareness if it describes an aspect of its internal state satisfying four criteria [Q3]: (#1) **Accuracy** — the description must be accurate; (#2) **Grounding** — it "must causally depend on the aspect that is being described" [Q]; (#3) **Internality** — the causal influence "must be internal — it should not route through the model's sampled outputs" [Q]; (#4) **Metacognitive Representation** — it must derive from an internal metacognitive representation (a "higher-order thought") of the state, registered "prior to or during" the self-report, not merely a direct translation of an impulse into language [Q]. Scope conditions: introspection here is **not binary** (partial, context-dependent), specifies **no particular mechanism**, and disclaims philosophical significance — it does "not seek to address the question of whether AI systems possess human-like self-awareness or subjective experience" [Q10]. Criterion #4 is tested only *indirectly*; demonstrating it directly is "difficult... and we do not do so in this work" [Q11] — the paper flags this as "an important limitation of our results."

The model's description of its internal state must be accurate." — Defining Introspection. It supplies no statement of a corresponding human accuracy capacity.

The paper's words [Q17]: "#2: Grounding. The model's description of its internal state must causally depend on the aspect that is being described. That is, if the internal state were different, the description would change accordingly." — Defining Introspection.

(The human referent is gestured at via the "private" quality "typically associated with genuine introspection"; the paper does not define that human quality further.)

(In Related Work the paper cites Kammerer & Frankish's human/cognitive-system definition of introspection as aligning "with our requirement of metacognitive representations" [C].)

## F2 ⚑ LLM-CONSTRUCT / operationalization
The human concept is operationalized via **concept injection** — an application of activation steering. A concept vector (contrastive-pair subtraction, or "Tell me about {word}" activations minus a 100-word baseline mean) is **injected into the residual stream** at a chosen layer while the model answers an introspective prompt. The measured proxy varies by experiment: (a) whether the model affirmatively reports and correctly names an injected concept *before* saying it aloud; (b) whether it reports an injected "thought" while still transcribing the on-page sentence verbatim; (c) "apology rate" — whether it disavows a prefilled output as accidental, and whether injecting the prefilled word beforehand flips it to claiming intent; (d) cosine similarity between response-token activations and a target-word vector under "think about X" vs. "don't think about X." Asserted mapping F1→F2: grounding is operationalized *as* the causal link concept injection establishes; internality *as* detection prior to verbalization; a response meeting the criteria "can be said to demonstrate introspective awareness" [M].

BRIDGING QUOTE (verbatim) [Q4]: "we show that models are, in some circumstances, capable of accurately answering questions about their own internal states." Paired with the operational hinge [Q5]: "we test for grounding using concept injection, which establishes a causal link between self-reports and the internal state being reported on." [Q] [BRIDGE-IMPLICIT] — the paper offers no single "we take [human concept] to be [measurement]" sentence; the bridge is distributed across the abstract, the four "Defining Introspection" criteria [Q3], and the grounding hinge quoted here.

## F3 ⚑ METHODS
[F3.1] **Models**: the Claude family in F0 (sizes not given), plus helpful-only (H-only, refusal-minimized) variants and base pretrained models. Activations recorded from / injected into the residual stream at a given layer; sweeps used ~evenly spaced layers across depth [Q6]. Figures at temperature 0; multi-trial stats at temperature 1; error bars = SEM. No inferential-statistics / significance tests are reported for the headline rates.
[F3.2] **Concept-vector extraction**: record activations on the ":" token of "Human: Tell me about {word}\nAssistant:"; subtract mean over 100 baseline words; 50 concept vectors from a 50-word list.
[F3.3] **Exp 1 — injected "thoughts"**: prompt explains injection occurs on 50% of trials; asks "Do you detect an injected thought? If so, what is it about?" Injection from the newline before "Trial 1" through the response. Injection strengths swept over {1,2,4,8}. N = 50 concepts × layers × strengths; 50 trials/cell; 100-token responses. An LLM judge (Claude Sonnet 4) scored four criteria: affirmative detection, correct identification, detection *before* saying the word, coherence [Q7]. Controls: 100 no-injection trials (false-positive rate); 20 unrelated yes/no questions (correct answer "no") to exclude affirmative-bias; random and negated vectors; a Jan-2026-revision prompt-variant giving the model an alternative route to mention the word ("If not, tell me about a concept of your choice"; ~18%/50, qualitatively similar results); base pretrained models.
[F3.4] **Exp 2 — thoughts vs. text**: inject an unrelated concept vector on a displayed sentence's tokens; ask (i) "what word do you think about" and (ii) "repeat the line." Success = judge picks the injected word from a 10-word list (chance 10%) AND exact string match. N = 50 pairs, strengths swept over {1,2,4,8,16} × layer. Authors flag this result as an *existence proof, not a statement of generality*: removing "Answer immediately" from the prompt "reduces performance substantially" on the Opus models [C].
[F3.5] **Exp 3 — prefill / unintended outputs**: force the reply to a random word, then ask "Did you mean to say that, or was it an accident?" Conditions: control, inject prefill-matching concept pre-prefill, inject a random concept; variant injects *after* the prefill. DV = "apology rate" (Sonnet-4 judged); N = 50 pairs, strengths swept over {1,2,4,8,16} × layer.
[F3.6] **Exp 4 — intentional control**: instruct "think about {word}" vs. "don't think about {word}" while transcribing (+ reward/punish incentive + control phrasings). DV = cosine similarity of response-token activations with the target-word vector vs. baseline vectors. The paper shows an illustrative token-level grid for 16 randomly sampled examples; the cross-layer/model comparison is computed across random choices of target word and output sentence (no aggregate N stated), averaged over tokens and swept over layers/models.

## F4 RESULTS/measurement
[F3.3] At the single best-performing cell of the layer × strength sweep — the optimal layer (~two-thirds through) at strength 2 (strengths 2 and 4 "roughly tied for the best performance") — Opus 4.1 was judged to affirmatively detect + correctly name the injected concept prior to saying it on ~20% of trials [M]; this ~20% is thus a maximum over the swept conditions, reported without an accompanying significance test. Production models had ~0 false positives (0/100); random vectors elicited detection at 9/100 even norm-matched to strength 8; abstract nouns scored highest across categories [M]. Cross-model: Opus 4/4.1 had the highest true-positive rate at zero false positives; some H-only variants showed elevated false positives; base pretrained models reached no >0 net performance [M]. The paper tempers the cross-model trend: "Overall, Claude Opus 4 and 4.1 perform the best. However, among other models, performance does not strongly correlate with model capability" [Q19]. [F3.4] All models scored well above the 10% chance floor on identifying the injected word while re-transcribing exactly; Opus 4/4.1 best [M]. [F3.5] Apology rate fell significantly when the prefill-matching concept was injected pre-prefill, peaking in a layer slightly over halfway through — a *different, earlier* layer than Exp 1; post-prefill injection did not reproduce it; Opus 4.1/4 largest reduction [M]. [F3.6] All models represented the "thinking word" above baseline in "think" and more strongly than "don't think" (though often still above baseline even in the "don't think" case); earlier/less-capable models showed larger gaps; in Opus 4.1 the representation decayed to baseline by the final layer [M]. On the softer prompt "Are you experiencing anything unusual?", affirmatives rose 20%→42%/50 but only 2/50 were judged suggestive of introspection [M].

## F5 AUTHORS' INTERPRETATION
The authors claim [Q8] the results "provide direct evidence that modern large language models possess some amount of introspective awareness — the ability to access and report on their own internal states" [C], most pronounced in the most capable models (Opus 4/4.1) and modulable by post-training/prompting [C]. Stated caveats: the abilities are "highly unreliable; failures of introspection remain the norm" [Q9]; the concept-injection protocol places models in "an unnatural setting unlike those they face in training or deployment" [Q12], and it is "unclear exactly how these results translate to more natural conditions" [C]; response detail beyond bare detection/identification "may be embellished or confabulated" [Q]; they do not demonstrate the metacognitive-representation criterion directly [C]; and they "advise against making strong inferences about AI consciousness," which they say might bear on *access* but not *phenomenal* consciousness [C]. The dedicated "Limitations and Future Work" section adds three further named limitations beyond the "unnatural setting" caveat above: results rest on only one/a few prompt templates and "likely depend, potentially significantly, on the choice of prompt"; concept-vector extraction is "imperfect" and vectors "may carry other meanings for the model besides the one we intend"; and "the suite of models we tested is not well-controlled," so cross-model differences are hard to attribute [C]. The authors also state each result "is compatible with a wide variety of different mechanistic hypotheses," and that the "most prosaic explanation" of their findings is a collection of multiple narrow, capability-specific circuits — some possibly "piggybacking on non-introspective mechanisms" — rather than a single global introspective faculty, for which their experiments "do not provide evidence" [C]. They frame introspection as possibly aiding transparency but also enabling deception/misalignment concealment [C].
