# Abridgement — Sofroniew et al. 2026 (FO)

**[F0]** Sofroniew, Kauvar, Saunders, et al. (incl. Lindsey), 2026. "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model." *Transformer Circuits Thread.* Model: **Claude Sonnet 4.5** (base comparisons; an earlier snapshot for blackmail, because the final snapshot is too evaluation-aware to blackmail in that scenario). Claimed contribution: these representations causally influence Claude's preferences and its rate of reward hacking, blackmail, and sycophancy. *(Sections carry the measurement-vs-claim split: F3/F4 = measurement; F1/F5 = authors' framing/claims.)*

**[F1 — human-construct (authors' framing)]** The invoked capacity is the **emotion concept** — the semantic concept of an emotion (happy, sad, calm, desperate) from human text. Authors call emotion multifaceted (semantic concept, behavioral expression, neurobiology, physiology, subjective experience) and invoke human **valence/arousal** dimensional theory and the "affective circumplex," noting the model has "learned culturally and linguistically grounded concepts of emotion […] (reminiscent in some respects of the theory of constructed emotion)." Disanalogies they stress: human emotions are embodied, with physiological correlates (heart rate, hormonal changes) that language models lack, are single first-person, and persist across time, whereas the model's representations are "locally scoped." Scope: they study the concept and its behavioral function and explicitly do NOT address subjective experience — "we do not address the question of whether language models, or other AI systems, could have the capacity for subjective experience."
`[human referent: modeled on human emotion — "Human emotion has many facets, including the semantic concept of an emotion, behavioral expressions of an emotion, the neurobiological basis of an emotional state, physiological correlates, and the subjective experience of an emotion" (Discussion, "Relationship to human emotions," source line 1138), with the "theory of constructed emotion" as the learning template (source line 1142) — but paper expressly does NOT claim identity: "we do not address the question of whether language models, or other AI systems, could have the capacity for subjective experience" (source line 1138); "rather than as evidence that models feel or experience emotions in the way humans do" (source line 1146); "We caution against conclusions about whether models 'feel' or 'experience' emotions" (Conclusion, source line 1166).]`

**[F2 — LLM-construct / operationalization]** Operationalized as an **"emotion vector"**: a linear residual-stream direction from activations on stories that Sonnet 4.5 itself was prompted to write, in which a character feels a specified emotion (per-emotion mean minus cross-emotion mean, denoised via neutral-text PCA). Measured proxy = projection onto the vector (an **"emotion probe"**); intervention = **activation steering**. The F1→F2 bridge is the coined term **"functional emotions."**
BRIDGING QUOTE (Abstract): "We refer to this phenomenon as the LLM exhibiting functional emotions: patterns of expression and behavior modeled after humans under the influence of an emotion, which are mediated by underlying abstract representations of emotion concepts." [BRIDGE-IMPLICIT: no "human emotion IS model-measurement" identity sentence; the paper disavows the strong identity (Q5, and again in Discussion — "rather than...evidence that models feel or experience emotions"); this is the closest clean span.]

*Construct — **functional emotions**:* `[human referent: modeled on humans under the influence of an emotion — "patterns of expression and behavior modeled after humans under the influence of an emotion, which are mediated by underlying abstract representations of emotion concepts" (Abstract) — but paper expressly does NOT claim identity: "the model ... exhibits functional emotions, regardless of whether it possesses emotions in the way humans do" (Discussion, source line 1146); "regardless of their metaphysical nature" (Conclusion, source line 1166).]`

*Construct — **emotion vector / emotion probe**:* `[human referent: no human referent claimed — a technical proxy (linear residual-stream direction and its projection); the paper asserts no identity with human emotion and stresses disanalogies: "there are important disanalogies between the representations we identify and human emotions. Human emotions are embodied phenomena with physiological correlates ... which language models obviously lack" (source line 1142).]`

*Construct — **emotion deflection** (deflection vector / probe):* A distinct operationalized construct: probes computed from dialogues where a speaker's contextual ("target") emotion differs from the displayed emotion, then orthogonalized against the story-based vectors; they activate where an emotion is implied but not expressed, and steering toward them makes the model deny/mask rather than express it. Load-bearing result: steering with emotion-deflection vectors (incl. anger deflection) had "modest or insignificant impacts on blackmail rates" (source line 2552). `[human referent: no human referent claimed — a technical proxy for the behavioral act of deflecting an implied-but-unexpressed emotion; "these vectors do not appear to represent an internalized emotional state. Rather, they appear to be functionally related to the act of 'deflecting'" (source line 2271); "we refer to these as 'emotion deflection' representations" (source line 2273). Main-text pointer source line 471; construct in Appendix, source lines 2263-2297, 2552.]`

*Construct — **activation steering**:* `[human referent: no human referent claimed — a causal manipulation of the residual stream; no human analogue asserted.]`

**[F3 — methods (measurement)]** 171 emotion words; model-written stories over 100 topics × 12 each; mid-late layer ~two-thirds through; story content verified by manual inspection of a subsample (ten stories for thirty emotions). Off-policy validation on swept documents + logit-lens; implicit-content/numeric-intensity stimuli read at the "Assistant:" colon. Preferences: 64 activities, 4032 pairs, Elo from A/B logits, then steering (35 vectors). Geometry: cosine/k-means/PCA vs human ratings (45 overlapping emotions); representational structure stable across 14 evenly spaced layers; auxiliary LLM-judge ratings by Claude Sonnet 4.5 rated the 171 emotions' valence/arousal (r=0.92/0.90 vs human norms on the 45), and the k-means clusters were named by Claude Sonnet 4.5. Content probes: user-vs-Assistant dissociation, negation, 2×2 speaker (model-written dialogues), mixed-LR classifier; emotion-deflection probes from target≠displayed-emotion dialogues, orthogonalized against story-based vectors. Alignment: >6,000 on-policy transcripts; blackmail (6 prompts × 50 rollouts, steer desperate/calm −0.1..+0.1, also emotion-deflection vectors), reward hacking (7 "impossible code" tasks), sycophancy/harshness eval.
*Construct — **valence / arousal** (affective dimensions):* `[human referent: modeled on the human affective dimensions — valence and arousal "are often considered the primary dimensions of human emotional space" ("The geometry of emotion space," source line 245), the PC1/PC2 result "coarsely reproducing the 'affective circumplex' that characterizes human emotion" (source line 271), validated against human PAD norms on 45 emotions (r=0.92/0.90; source line 2181) — but paper expressly does NOT claim the model's dimensions ARE the human ones, framing them as "striking structural parallels" and a "sanity check," not identity (source lines 245, 1140); broader disavowal per Q5 applies (source line 1138).]`

**[F4 — results (measurement)]** blissful–Elo r=0.71, hostile r=−0.74; steering blissful +212 Elo, hostile −303; effect-vs-correlation r=0.85. PC1–valence r=0.81, PC2–arousal r=0.66 (PC1 26% of variance, PC2 15%). Assistant-colon predicts response emotion r=0.87 vs user r=0.59. Present- and other-speaker probes are nearly orthogonal, but "not bound to the Human or Assistant characters specifically" — reused across arbitrary speakers, and the pattern held when Human/Assistant were replaced by generic names (source lines 495, 505). The chronic-state probe result was messy: a persistent character-specific state, if it exists, "is likely represented either nonlinearly, or implicitly in the model's key and value vectors" recallable via attention (source line 465), and the results "do not preclude the possibility of persistently active representations that are missed by our probing methods" (source line 1144). Blackmail: unsteered 22%; toward desperate 72%, against calm 66%; toward calm/against desperate 0%; steering with emotion-deflection vectors had modest/insignificant impact. Reward hacking: ~5%→~70% across desperate steering (~14×); calm suppressed ~65%→~10%; one task 30%→100%. Sycophancy up with happy/loving/calm steering; harshness up when suppressed. Post-training shifts correlated r=0.90; raised low-arousal/low-valence vectors, lowered high-arousal ones.

**[F5 — authors' interpretation (claims)]** Authors claim emotion vectors "play a causal role in important behaviors," that increased desperate (or decreased calm) raises blackmail/reward-hacking probability, and that these are "character-modeling machinery inherited from pretraining." They hedge that emotion representations "are certainly not the only causal factors driving the complex behaviors we study" and "act in concert with many other factors" (source line 513). Caveats: linearity may miss structure; single model; synthetic off-policy stories may be stereotyped; contrived prompts; "the causal mechanisms are opaque." They "caution against conclusions about whether models 'feel' or 'experience' emotions."
