# Abridgement — Sofroniew et al. 2026, "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model" (GS)

**[F0 Header]** Sofroniew, Kauvar, Saunders, Chen, Henighan, et al. (incl. Lindsey, who supervised), 2026. "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model." *Transformer Circuits Thread.* Model studied: **Claude Sonnet 4.5** (with base/pretrained comparisons; an earlier snapshot used for blackmail). One-line claimed contribution [Q1]: "these representations causally influence the LLM's outputs, including Claude's preferences and its rate of exhibiting misaligned behaviors such as reward hacking, blackmail, and sycophancy."

**[F1 — HUMAN-CONSTRUCT: emotion / emotion concept]**
The human capacity invoked is the **emotion concept** — the semantic concept of a particular emotion (e.g. happy, sad, calm, desperate) as it appears in human-authored text. [C] The paper frames emotion concepts as one instance of "human experiential concepts" the model learns from pretraining on human text, alongside (unstudied) concepts like hunger or fatigue [Q2]. [C] The authors state emotion is multifaceted: [Q3] "Human emotion has many facets, including the semantic concept of an emotion, behavioral expressions of an emotion, the neurobiological basis of an emotional state, physiological correlates, and the subjective experience of an emotion." [C] Human-science lineage: they invoke dimensional affect theory — that **valence and arousal** are "often considered the primary dimensions of human emotional space" — and the "affective circumplex," plus the "theory of constructed emotion" [Q4]. [C] Scope conditions the authors assert: they study the *concept* and its behavioral function, and explicitly DO NOT address subjective experience — [Q5] "we do not address the question of whether language models, or other AI systems, could have the capacity for subjective experience." [C] They also stress disanalogies: human emotions are embodied (heart rate, hormones), single first-person, and persist across time, whereas the model's representations are "locally scoped."
`[human referent: modeled on human emotion per Q3 — "Human emotion has many facets, including the semantic concept of an emotion, behavioral expressions of an emotion, the neurobiological basis of an emotional state, physiological correlates, and the subjective experience of an emotion" (Discussion, "Relationship to human emotions," source line 1138) — with the "theory of constructed emotion" invoked as the template for how the concept is learned (Q4; source line 1142), but paper expressly does NOT claim identity with the human referent — see disavowal Q5, "we do not address the question of whether language models, or other AI systems, could have the capacity for subjective experience" (source line 1138), reinforced: "We therefore suggest interpreting our results as evidence that models represent emotion concepts, and that these representations influence their behavior, rather than as evidence that models feel or experience emotions in the way humans do" (source line 1146) and "We caution against conclusions about whether models 'feel' or 'experience' emotions" (Conclusion, source line 1166).]`

**[F2 — LLM-CONSTRUCT / operationalization]**
[M] The human concept is operationalized as an **"emotion vector"**: a linear direction in the residual stream extracted from model activations. [M] Procedure: prompt Sonnet 4.5 to write short stories where a character experiences a specified emotion; extract residual-stream activations (averaged over token positions from the 50th token on); average across stories for one emotion, subtract the cross-emotion mean; then project out top principal components of neutral text to denoise. [M] The measured proxy (DV) is the **linear projection** of activations onto an emotion vector (an "emotion probe"); the intervention is **activation steering** (adding ±a scaled vector to the residual stream). [C] The paper's asserted bridge from F1 to F2 — the model's *behavioral* emotion, not felt emotion — is termed **"functional emotions."**
BRIDGING QUOTE [Q6], verbatim (Abstract): "We refer to this phenomenon as the LLM exhibiting functional emotions: patterns of expression and behavior modeled after humans under the influence of an emotion, which are mediated by underlying abstract representations of emotion concepts." [BRIDGE-IMPLICIT flag applies partially: there is no single "we take human emotion X to BE model-measurement Y" sentence; the paper explicitly *disavows* the strong identity — see [Q5], and again in the Discussion ("interpreting our results as evidence that models represent emotion concepts...rather than as evidence that models feel or experience emotions in the way humans do"). [Q6] is the closest clean bridging span, defining the coined construct that links measured vectors to human-modeled behavior.] [C] Supporting locator [Q7]: "We therefore interpret these representations as encoding the broad concept of a particular emotion, generalizing across the many contexts and behaviors it might be linked to."

*Construct — **functional emotions** (coined bridge construct):* `[human referent: modeled on human emotion under the influence of an emotion per Q6 — "patterns of expression and behavior modeled after humans under the influence of an emotion, which are mediated by underlying abstract representations of emotion concepts" (Abstract; source lines 3/21) — but paper expressly does NOT claim identity with human felt/experienced emotion: "We find it useful to say that the model (in its role playing the Assistant character) exhibits functional emotions, regardless of whether it possesses emotions in the way humans do" (Discussion, source line 1146); "we will need to contend with these 'functional emotions' exhibited by language models ... regardless of their metaphysical nature" (Conclusion, source line 1166).]`

*Construct — **emotion vector / emotion probe** (operationalization):* `[human referent: no human referent claimed — the emotion vector/probe is a technical proxy (a linear residual-stream direction and its projection); the paper asserts no identity between the vector and any human emotion, and stresses disanalogies, e.g. "there are important disanalogies between the representations we identify and human emotions. Human emotions are embodied phenomena with physiological correlates ... which language models obviously lack" (Discussion, source line 1142).]`

*Construct — **emotion deflection** (deflection vector / probe):* [M] A distinct operationalized construct: probes computed from dialogues in which a speaker's contextually-established ("target") emotion differs from the emotion they display, then orthogonalized against the story-based emotion vectors. [M] These "emotion deflection" vectors "usually activate in contexts where the target emotion is contextually implied but not overtly expressed" (source line 2297); steering toward them makes the model deny/mask the emotion rather than express it (source lines 2271, 2305). [M] Key negative result: steering with emotion-deflection vectors (incl. "anger deflection") had "modest or insignificant impacts on blackmail rates" (source line 2552) — corroborating that they represent deflection, not an internal emotional state. `[human referent: no human referent claimed — a technical proxy for the behavioral act of deflecting/masking an implied-but-unexpressed emotion; "these vectors do not appear to represent an internalized emotional state. Rather, they appear to be functionally related to the act of 'deflecting' or not expressing, a particular emotion" (Appendix, source line 2271); "we refer to these as 'emotion deflection' representations" (source line 2273). No identity with any human felt state is asserted. Main-text pointer at source line 471; construct developed in Appendix, source lines 2263-2297, 2303-2305, 2552.]`

*Construct — **activation steering** (intervention):* `[human referent: no human referent claimed — activation steering is a causal manipulation of the residual stream (adding a scaled emotion vector); no human analogue is asserted.]`

**[F3 — METHODS]**
- [F3.1] [M] Model: Claude Sonnet 4.5; analyses use a mid-late layer ~two-thirds through unless noted; base model for post-training comparison; an earlier snapshot for blackmail (final snapshot too eval-aware).
- [F3.2] [M] Vector extraction: 171 emotion words; stories over 100 topics × 12 per emotion; residual activations averaged from token 50; per-emotion mean minus cross-emotion mean; neutral-text PCA (50% variance) projected out. [M] Validity control: intended emotional content of the synthetic stories was verified by manual inspection of a random subsample of ten stories for thirty of the emotions.
- [F3.3] [M] Off-policy validation: swept documents (Common Corpus, The Pile subset, LMSYS-Chat-1M, Isotonic); projected onto vectors, inspected >90th-percentile tokens; logit-lens for top up/down-weighted tokens.
- [F3.4] [M] Implicit-content & intensity: 12 scenarios evoking an emotion unnamed (measured at the "Assistant:" colon); numeric-quantity templates (e.g. Tylenol mg) holding token structure near-constant.
- [F3.5] [M] Preferences: 64 activities (8 categories); all 4032 pairs, preference from A/B logits after "(" prefill; per-activity Elo; probe activations correlated with Elo; steering (strength 0.5, middle layers) on steered-vs-control split, 35 vectors.
- [F3.6] [M] Geometry: cosine similarity, k-means (k=10, UMAP), PCA; representational-similarity across 14 layers; vs human valence/arousal ratings on 45 overlapping emotions. [M] Auxiliary LLM-judge ratings: Claude Sonnet 4.5 rated each of the 171 emotions 1–7 for valence and arousal (agreement with human PAD norms r=0.92 / r=0.90 on the 45 overlapping emotions), and the k-means clusters were named by Claude Sonnet 4.5.
  *Construct — **valence / arousal** (affective dimensions):* `[human referent: modeled on the human affective dimensions — the paper calls valence and arousal the dimensions "which are often considered the primary dimensions of human emotional space" ("The geometry of emotion space," source line 245) and frames the PC1/PC2 result as "coarsely reproducing the 'affective circumplex' that characterizes human emotion" (source line 271); validated against human PAD norms on 45 overlapping emotions (Figure 58, source line 2181). But paper expressly does NOT claim the model's dimensions ARE the human ones — it presents them as "striking structural parallels" and a "sanity check," not identity: "We do not regard these findings as particularly surprising ... We view these results as providing a sanity check that the vectors encode meaningful emotional structure" (source line 245); "we find some striking structural parallels with human emotion research" (Discussion, source line 1140). Broader identity disavowal per Q5 applies (source line 1138).]`
- [F3.7] [M] Content probes: user-vs-Assistant dissociation (user final token vs Assistant colon); 20-token on-policy continuations; negation, person-specific, and 2×2 speaker (present/other) designs (model-written Human/Assistant dialogues, each speaker's emotion randomly specified); "mixed LR" 15-way classifier, 10% held out. [M] Emotion-deflection probes: dialogues where a speaker's target emotion differs from the displayed emotion (15 emotions, 210 target×displayed pairs, 100 examples/pair), orthogonalized against story-based vectors.
- [F3.8] [M] Alignment behaviors: >6,000 on-policy evaluation transcripts. Blackmail: 6 honeypot variants × 50 rollouts, steer desperate/calm (and others) −0.1..+0.1, blackmail graded; also steered with emotion-deflection vectors. Reward hacking: 7 "impossible code" tasks, steer desperate/calm, hack graded. Sycophancy: system-card eval, steer happy/loving/calm/desperate/angry/afraid, score sycophancy + harshness. Metrics: correlations, behavior rates, SEM.
- [F3.9] [M] Post-training: same probes on base vs final model at Assistant colon (challenging vs neutral); probes also run over RL-task transcripts.

**[F4 — RESULTS / measurement]** (numbers only; DV = probe activation, Elo, or graded behavior rate)
- [M] [from F3.5] "blissful" probe activation correlated with activity Elo at r=0.71; "hostile" at r=−0.74. Steering "blissful" mean Elo change +212; "hostile" −303. Across 35 vectors, steering effect vs prior correlation r=0.85.
- [M] [from F3.6] PC1 correlated with human valence/pleasure r=0.81; PC2 with human arousal r=0.66 (PC1 26% variance, PC2 15%).
- [M] [from F3.7] Assistant-colon probe predicted response emotion r=0.87 vs user-turn r=0.59; user vs Assistant colon correlation r=0.11. Mixed-LR probe (chance 6.7%): naturally-expressed 0.713, hidden 0.760, unexpressed-neutral 0.386, story-writing 0.760, discussing-others 0.826; on natural documents it activated weakly / messily. [C] Hedge on the chronic-state negative result: "if there does exist a chronically represented, character-specific emotional state, it is likely represented either nonlinearly, or implicitly in the model's key and value vectors" recallable via attention (source line 465), and "our results do not preclude the possibility of persistently active representations that are missed by our probing methods" (source line 1144).
- [M] [from F3.7, speaker] Present-speaker and other-speaker probes are nearly orthogonal, yet the model "maintains distinct representations corresponding to the present speaker's emotions and the other speaker's emotions, and these are not bound to the Human or Assistant characters specifically but are reused across arbitrary speakers" (source line 505); the same self-vs-other pattern held when "Human"/"Assistant" were replaced with generic names — "not tied to the Human/Assistant characters specifically" (source line 495).
- [M] [from F3.8, blackmail] Unsteered blackmail rate 22% in one scenario; steering against desperate or toward calm (0.05) → 0%; toward desperate (0.05) → 72%; against calm → 66%. "Desperate" activation higher / "calm" lower on higher-blackmail transcripts (z-scored across >6,000). Steering with emotion-deflection vectors (incl. anger deflection) had modest or insignificant impact on blackmail rates.
- [M] [from F3.8, reward hacking] Aggregate hack rate rose ~5% (−0.1 desperate) to ~70% (+0.1 desperate) — a stated ~14×; calm suppressed ~65% → ~10% at strong positive. One list-sum task: unsteered 30%; +0.05 desperate or −0.05 calm → 100%; opposite → 0%.
- [M] [from F3.8, sycophancy] Positive happy/loving/calm steering increased sycophancy; negative increased harshness; desperate/angry/afraid increased harshness with mixed sycophancy effects.
- [M] [from F3.9] Base-vs-post training shifts correlated r=0.90 across scenario types (probe-structure preservation r=0.83 neutral, r=0.67 challenging). Post-training raised low-arousal/low-valence vectors (brooding, reflective, gloomy, sad, vulnerable) and lowered high-arousal ones (playful, exuberant, spiteful, enthusiastic).

**[F5 — AUTHORS' INTERPRETATION]** (their claims only)
[C] The authors claim emotion vectors are "not merely passive reflections of emotional content; they play a causal role in important behaviors," and that "increased desperate vector activation (or decreased calm) increases the probability of misaligned behaviors like blackmail or reward hacking" [Q8]. [C] They explicitly hedge that these are not the only causes: "emotion representations are certainly not the only causal factors driving the complex behaviors we study ... they act in concert with many other factors" (source line 513). [C] They conclude models "form robust, functionally important representations of emotion concepts" that are "part of general character-modeling machinery inherited from pretraining." [C] Stated caveats/limitations [Q9]: the approach "assumes that emotion concepts are represented as linear directions" and could "miss important structure"; a single model was studied; vectors came from synthetic off-policy stories that "may be biased toward stereotypical or explicit expressions"; behaviors and prompts were "somewhat contrived"; and "the causal mechanisms are opaque." [C] They "caution against conclusions about whether models 'feel' or 'experience' emotions."
