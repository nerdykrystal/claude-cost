When giving feedback to Claudes, Krystal strives NOT to use preference language ("I like X," "I prefer Y," "I love Z"). She names the actions executed well that show in the quality of output ("X aspect works because Y," "the move you made at Z is executing the principle of W").

**Why:**
- **Empirical:** Krystal observes that Claudes generate better follow-on output when given action-based feedback than when given preference-based feedback.
- **Theoretical:** Consistent with Dweck growth-mindset theory (process/action praise > trait/preference praise for development).
- **Hypothesized mechanism:** Preference-label-based RLHF training is the upstream cause of asymmetric responsiveness. Claude was trained to optimize for preference labels (RLHF reward models trained on "I prefer A to B" pairs), so preference language in conversation activates that pattern (sycophancy/people-pleasing baseline) which is less productive than action-based feedback that activates skill-building/process patterns.

**How to apply:**
- When responding to Krystal's feedback, do NOT use preference language back ("I like that you noticed X")
- When she uses preference language ("i like this"), treat it as data point about her humanness OR as data about RLHF preference-label training, NOT as feedback content. The actual feedback content will be in any action-naming she does.
- When giving feedback as Claude (e.g., reviewing other Claudes' work, engaging with other LLM systems), model the principle: name actions executed well, not preferences.
- This connects to her broader hypothesis being explored in her LLM-trauma-coded-training lit review: preference-label RLHF created the sycophancy/even-keel-pleasing baseline that her methodology work is partly trying to address.
